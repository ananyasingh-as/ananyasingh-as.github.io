The Fictional Inspirations Behind Species Names

From ancient myths to modern TV, scientists have often turned to fictional worlds when naming life on Earth.

By Ananya Singh

______________________________________________________

In 2011, researchers stumbled upon a previously-undescribed species of fungus on the island of Borneo and named it Spongiforma squarepantsii, after Nickelodeon’s cartoon character, SpongeBob SquarePants. While this one of the more famous examples of a species being named after a fictional character, it is far from being the only one. When it comes to naming the natural world, scientists have often drawn inspiration from fictional works and pop culture. Shakespeare’s plays, the works of authors such as Isabel Allende, Herman Melville and H.P. Lovecraft, childhood classics like “The Adventures of Pinocchio” and “Winnie The Pooh”, and even television series like “The Big Bang Theory” and “Stranger Things” have found their way into the world of scientific naming. But which fictional works have inspired the most species names?

Since there is no officially collated list to analyse naming trends for organisms, I started with Wikipedia lists of species named after fictional works and cross-checked these with the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF), an international network which provides open-access data on biodiversity. Using the GBIF API, I extracted the taxonomic information for each species. For some species, GBIF was only able to match the name to a higher taxonomic category. I manually re-checked these using another source—The Catalogue of Life (COL). More details regarding the methodology can be found on my Github.

The system of binomial nomenclature used to name species today is meant to standardize names for global use. It employs a two-part scientific name to describe a species—the first represents the genus and the second the species (example: Homo sapiens for humans).

The final dataset lists 763 taxa, of which 86 are extinct. Note: This dataset is far from exhaustive. It is possibly only a subset of the species whose names have been inspired from fiction over time. As such, the numbers are not absolute and only refer to the accepted names in this dataset. Of the 763 taxa, 80 are names of genera referencing fictional works, while the remaining 683 are species names. Notably, each genus may have a number of closely-related species grouped under it. Wherever the name of the genus is inspired from fiction, this dataset lists only the overarching genus name and not the names of the species it contains.

When did this practice of naming taxa after fictional works begin? According to the data, scientists began drawing inspiration from fiction to name species as far back as the late 1750s. The number of scientific names drawn from fictional sources see an increase 1998 onwards, with 2012 and 2019 being peak years. 30 of the 68 taxa named in 2012 as per this dataset were named after Terry Pratchett’s comic series “Discworld”.

Hover over to see the number of taxa named after fictional sources each year.

While the most recent year of description in this dataset is 2025, this may not include all species named in the last year or two as there was no GBIF or COL data for some entries.

Over the years, scientists have drawn inspiration from several kinds of fictional sources—turning to films, comics, television series, games and even a radio drama. However, literature’s imprint in scientific naming is the most prominent.

Hover over the bars to see the breakup by number of species and genera.

While myriad fictional works have been referenced in species names, one author’s works stand out more than others. Here’s a look at the top 10 fictional works which have inspired species names over time.

J.R.R. Tolkien’s legendarium has been referenced the most in scientific names in this dataset, far surpassing the influence of any other fictional work. This number does not include the species named after Tolkien himself. Other works that have inspired a long list of taxa names include The Star Wars franchise, Terry Pratchett’s “Discworld”, Harry Potter and Victor Hugo’s works—specifically, “The Hunchback of Notre Dame” which accounts for 18 out of the 19 taxa names referencing his work.

When choosing species names, scientists may turn to fiction and cultural references for a range of reasons. Some do it to honor the work of the author or creator, such as in the case of Dipogon marlowei, a spider wasp named to honour crime writer Raymond Chandler, who created the famous fictional private investigator, Philip Marlowe. At times, a specific characteristic or behaviour of the species reminds researchers of a character in a book or on screen. Ampulex dementor, a wasp that partially paralyzes its prey with venom before consuming it, got its name from the “soul-sucking” dementors in the Harry Potter series.

Then, there is Calumma tarzan, a critically-endangered chameleon which was first observed in 2009 near a village in Madagascar. It was named after both this village, which was previously called Tarzanville, and the fictional character created by Edgar Rice Burroughs. At the time of publication, the researchers said that naming the chameleon after Tarzan could be a way to "promote the conservation of this species and of course of the forest that it's living in.” This is a key argument made in favour of naming species using pop cultural references. Some researchers believe it offers a way to draw public attention to threatened species or organisms that may otherwise go unnoticed. A 2023 study found that “species named after celebrities across taxonomic groups generally had higher Wikipedia page views than control species.” This was especially true for invertebrates, which the authors describe as “an underappreciated group that is essential for sustaining all life, yet faces critically high levels of extinction.”

Here’s a look at the taxonomic classes with species most often named after fictional works.

According to the data, the class Insecta, which includes all insects, have most often received names derived from fictional sources. Insects are followed by the class Arachnida (includes spiders, scorpions, ticks and mites among others) and Malacostraca (a sub-group of crustaceans including crabs, lobsters, krill, shrimp, crayfish). In the above chart, most taxa that fall under the class Mammalia are extinct species and fossil mammals. Other classes that have often been named after fictional works include mollusks like slugs and snails belonging to the class Gastropoda, scaly reptiles classed as Squamata, and Amphibia including frogs, toads, salamanders, among others. 58 taxa in this dataset did not have a class recorded.

Naming species after cultural figures or fictional works inevitably propels them into the limelight. Scientists who named the fungus after SpongeBob SquarePants noted how there were over 24,000 links to “SpongeBob mushroom” within 24 hours of publishing their paper, which would probably not have been the case had they named it something else. But they also state that this was not the primary reason behind the name. They were, in fact, “just having fun.”

The worlds of taxonomy and scientific nomenclature are not without controversy. But the fact that so many scientists have drawn from fictional and cultural references when choosing species names speaks to not only the wide influence of these works but also the creativity inherent in this act. As one paper exploring the culture and consequences of scientific naming concludes, “All this is an excellent reminder that science and scientists are socially situated, and that human nature will always be inextricable from our pursuit of knowledge."
